# Color Palette

## Purpose of this file

This document defines the color logic of **New Age Glam Fantasy**.

Color is one of the fastest ways to make this style feel instantly right or instantly wrong. Before a viewer consciously notices the motifs, wardrobe, or analog texture, they often register the palette first.

---

## Core color statement

The color system of New Age Glam Fantasy is **prismatic, high-chroma, soft-textured, candy-radiant, and diffusion-friendly**.

It should **not** be treated as a pastel-only palette.

Its strongest form combines:
- pastel atmospheres
- Lisa Frank–adjacent saturation logic
- glam cosmetic color intensity
- rainbow refraction
- pearlescent haze
- VHS-soft color bleed
- secondary-color dominance

The palette should feel like **candy light passing through crystal, mist, satin, and analog tape memory**.

---

## Master palette identity

At its core, the palette combines:
- **soft pastel atmospheres**
- **bright candy-pop accents**
- **pink-forward glamour**
- **violet and orchid depth**
- **teal / aqua / turquoise energy**
- **lemon and peach light**
- **opal, pearl, and silver neutrals**
- **rainbow prism transitions**
- **analog softness that rounds the edges**

---

## Palette preference bias

This style system favors a **Lisa Frank–adjacent color bias** over a muted or strictly pastel New Age palette.

That means the palette usually prefers:
- **pink over red**
- **violet, orchid, or lavender over royal blue**
- **teal, aqua, or turquoise over standard blue**
- **coral-pink over orange-red**
- **lemon glow over mustard**
- **mint over dark green**
- **iridescent rainbow blends over single-hue seriousness**

---

## The five palette laws

### 1. Color must feel illuminated, not merely chosen
The colors should seem to come from glowing sources, refracted light, cosmetic shine, mist, reflective surfaces, or aura fields.

### 2. Saturation is welcome, but edge hardness is not
This style can be bright, loud, and poppy. But the color should still feel softened by haze, optical diffusion, VHS bloom, airbrush softness, or reflective blending.

**High chroma, soft edges**

### 3. Secondary-color dominance gives the palette its personality
This style is strongest when it leans into pinks, violets, teals, aquas, mints, orchids, and lemon glow instead of primary red / blue / yellow logic.

### 4. Rainbow logic should feel prismatic, not childish-flat
Rainbow can absolutely belong here, but it should feel like:
- crystal refraction
- airbrushed gradients
- aura spill
- candy fog
- holographic shimmer
- iridescent light behavior

### 5. Softness must coexist with contrast
The palette should not be washed out just because it is dreamy.

This style thrives on contrast like:
- hot pink against aqua
- violet against lemon glow
- lavender against teal
- magenta against pearl white
- coral-pink against mint
- turquoise against orchid

## Signature color combinations

### Combination 1: Hot Pink Signature
**Hot pink + orchid + teal + pearl white**

One of the best all-purpose signatures. Strong glam energy, spiritual fantasy mood, and clear pop.

### Combination 2: Celestial Airy
**Lavender + aqua + lemon glow + silver**

Airy, celestial, and a little more temple-like or visionary.

### Combination 3: Electric Contrast
**Magenta + turquoise + opal white**

Sharper and more electric, but still on-model if softened with haze.

### Combination 4: Sweet Romance
**Coral-pink + violet + mint + champagne**

Sweet, soft, and slightly more romantic.

### Combination 5: Maximalist Playful
**Bubblegum pink + lilac + aqua + rainbow prism flare**

Very strong for maximalist, playful, iconic pieces.

### Combination 6: Balanced Editorial
**Peach glow + orchid + teal + reflective white**

Balanced warm/cool palette with a beauty-editorial edge.

## Neutral support colors

Even a loud palette needs stabilizers.

The best neutrals for this style are:
- pearl white
- opal white
- moon silver
- champagne
- pale cream haze
- icy gray
- reflective white
- midnight violet
- glossy black (used sparingly)
- reflective black water

These neutrals should support the palette without muting it.

---

## Colors to deprioritize

These colors are not forbidden, but they are usually weaker anchors:

- primary red
- royal blue
- navy-heavy blue
- dark forest green
- muddy olive
- burnt orange
- mustard-heavy yellow
- beige-brown earth palettes
- grayscale-dominant schemes

**Why:** These colors often push the work toward generic fantasy, patriotic primaries, medieval illustration, earthy bohemian spirituality, sci-fi seriousness, or muted editorial restraint.

## Common color mistakes

### 1. Making the palette too pale
Dreamy does not mean weak. This style often wants bright, joyful, candy-radiant intensity.

### 2. Making the palette too primary
Too much red, blue, and yellow can flatten the mood into something more generic or childish in the wrong way.

### 3. Making the palette too synthwave
Hard sunset gradients, black-heavy contrast, and rigid neon geometry push the image away from metaphysical glam softness.

### 4. Making the palette too cyberpunk
Acid neon, dark urban contrast, and tech-noir blue-red logic break the spiritual glamour tone.

### 5. Making the palette too earthy
Browns, olives, rust, and dusty natural fibers can weaken the celestial candy-mystic feel.

### 6. Making rainbow too flat
Rainbow should feel refracted, airbrushed, misted, holographic, or glowing, not printed as simple stripes.

### 7. Making the palette too clean
Without bloom, haze, halation, color bleed, or analog softness, even a correct hue set can feel wrong.
